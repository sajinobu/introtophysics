WHAT IS PHYSICS?

Physics is the branch of science that studies the fundamental principles governing the universe.

It explores the nature and properties of matter and energy, as well as their interactions, leading to various observable phenomena.

ORIGIN OF THE TERM

The word Physics comes from the Greek term "fusis" (φύσις), which means "nature" or "natural things."

This reflects the essence of physics as the study of how the natural world works.

PHYSICS AS A SCIENCE

Physics is a Natural Science that aims to describe and explain the behavior of the physical world. It is both:

Descriptive
It observes and explains natural phenomena.
Predictive
It formulates mathematical models and theories to anticipate future outcomes.

EVOLUTION OF PHYSICS

The development of physics spans thousands of years, evolving through various key periods, each marked by groundbreaking discoveries that shaped our understanding of the universe.

Ancient Physics

The origins of physics can be traced back to early philosophers and scientists who sought to explain natural phenomena.

Aristotle

Proposed ideas about motion and the elements (earth, water, air, fire).

Archimedes

Developed principles of buoyancy and levers, laying the foundation for mechanics.

Ptolemy

Created the geocentric model of the universe, which dominated astronomy for centuries.

Classical Physics (16th–19th Century)

This period saw the formulation of precise mathematical laws governing motion, forces, and energy.

Galileo Galilei

Conducted experiments on motion and laid the groundwork for Newtonian mechanics.

Isaac Newton

Formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, revolutionizing mechanics.

James Clerk Maxwell

Developed the theory of electromagnetism, unifying electricity, magnetism, and light.

Modern Physics (20th Century–Present)

The 20th century introduced groundbreaking theories that challenged classical physics, leading to a deeper understanding of the microscopic and cosmic realms.

Albert Einstein

Developed the theory of relativity, fundamentally changing concepts of space, time, and gravity.

Niels Bohr

Contributed to atomic structure and quantum theory.

Werner Heisenberg

Formulated the uncertainty principle in quantum mechanics.

Erwin Schrödinger

Developed wave mechanics, a key component of quantum physics.

Richard Feynman

Advanced quantum electrodynamics, contributing to modern particle physics.

Stephen Hawking

Made significant contributions to black hole physics and cosmology.

Contemporary Physics and Beyond

Today, physics continues to evolve with new discoveries and advancements in technology.
